Agroforestry for a climate-resilient Ireland

Emerald Roots

Student-led agroforestry across Ireland

Emerald Roots connects students, farmers and communities to plant diverse native trees, restore soils and trial climate-smart farming on real Irish farms.


From hedgerows to small woodland pockets, we focus on practical, farmer-friendly agroforestry that fits within Irish schemes and supports both biodiversity and livelihoods.


Ireland was once almost completely blanketed in native woodland. Today, we have some of the lowest levels of native forest cover in Europe. Restoring these ecosystems matters‚Äînot just for biodiversity, but for climate resilience and rural livelihoods. Planting purely coniferous plantations may offer short-term timber gains, but it often leads to soil acidification, poor water quality, and limited habitat diversity. Instead, we champion mixed native species that rebuild layered canopies, healthy soils, and thriving wildlife corridors.

Why agroforestry matters in Ireland

Agroforestry blends trees, crops and livestock on the same piece of land. On Irish farms this can mean adding native trees into field boundaries, wet corners, around yards or along watercourses rather than taking whole fields out of production.

When designed well, agroforestry can work alongside schemes like ACRES and the Native Woodland Establishment Grant and the European Erasmus plus scheme, where your donations can help:

  • Plant native species such as oak, alder, birch, rowan and hazel in the right place.
  • Create shelter for livestock and reduce wind stress on grass and crops.
  • Protect streams and rivers with treed buffers that filter nutrients and sediment.
  • Reconnect fragmented habitats to support birds, bats and pollinators.
  • Build long-term resilience to heavier rain, summer drought and new pests.

Our pilot projects in the west of Ireland work with family farms to co-design small, manageable planting plans. Students help with mapping, monitoring and citizen-science style surveys so that each site becomes a living classroom for climate resilience, soil health and biodiversity.

Volunteer planting and forest maintanance days are also a great social excercise for meeting new people, connecting with nature and clearing the head, where the concept of forest bathing has been well known in Japan for centuries.
